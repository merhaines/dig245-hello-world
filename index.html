L'dor v'dor is not just about knowing the broader history but also understanding and learning from the personal experiences within individual families and communities. This principle emphasizes the duty to remember and transmit the collective memory of the Jewish people, ensuring that the atrocities of the Holocaust are never forgotten. It involves teaching the younger generations about the painful history, the loss of lives, and the resilience of survivors.

“Memorial Candle.” Arcadia Apaches Candlelight Memorial, 4 December 2021.

Art Spiegelman's Maus embodies the principle of L'dor v'dor by intricately weaving the personal experiences of the Spiegelman family with the broader historical context of the Holocaust, serving as a poignant testament to the duty of remembering and transmitting the collective memory of the Jewish people across generations.

There are many generations represented in Maus as the story recounts the Holocaust experiences of Vladek Spiegelman, a Polish Jew, and his son Art's struggles to understand his father's past. Vladek shares his harrowing survival story, detailing the impact of the Holocaust on their family and the transmission of generational trauma.

Thomasg. Art and Vladek Spiegelman mid 1950s.” I Need Art and Coffee, 8 May, 2017.

Art's decision to tell his father's story and depict it graphically in Maus is an active engagement with the concept of "L’dor v’dor." By documenting Vladek's experiences, Art is ensuring that the history and legacy of the Holocaust are not lost but are instead passed down to subsequent generations.

Dreifus, Claudia. “Drawing Is Always a Struggle: An Interview with Art Spiegelman.” nybooks.com, 13 April, 2018.

"My Father Bleeds History" signifies the transmission of information and experiences through the bloodline, embodying the essence of L'dor V'dor. It serves as a visceral acknowledgment by Spiegelman that the impact of violence persisted beyond the wartime events, leaving a lasting mark on subsequent generations.

In his publication titled “Necessary Stains,” Michael G. Levine guides readers on engaging with Maus, characterizing Art's recording of Vladek's narrative and readers' subsequent exploration of these accounts as a profound "act of belated witnessing," suggesting that as long as future generations bear witness, they become an integral part of the narrative, ensuring the story never dies.

Levine coins the term "internal bleeding," akin to Art's "My Father Bleeds History," highlighting how the events in Maus, particularly Vladek's story, aren't isolated; they metaphorically bleed together, forming a continuous and interconnected historical narrative that extends beyond the Holocaust, impacting both present and future generations.
